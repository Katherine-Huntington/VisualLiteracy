Shapes

Shapes are outlines of objects or parts of objects. There are two types of shapes: organic (natural) and geometric (man-made). Each kind of shape conveys their own unique meanings, so it's important to pick the right shapes to match the purpose of your design.

Organic Shapes

Organic shapes are relaxed, fluid, and natural, and tend to have curved lines and asymmetry. As such, they're generally much less solid, stable or precise than man-made geometric shapes. Think of plants, animals, rocks, rivers, flowers, or clouds, or literally anything else.

Geometric Shapes

Geometric shapes are man-made, mechanical, and mathematical, possessing smooth edges that are easy to measure. They can indicate solidity or rigidity. In graphic design, the three fundamental geometric shapes are the circle, square, and triangle. All other geometric shapes in graphic design are based around these, and each one can convey different meanings.

  • Squares and rectangles are the most stable shape of the three, and generally the most stagnant. This may explain the design of the “pause” button on the TV remote. Squares give a sense of order, reliability, structure, and trustworthiness, and have quite the industrial, man-made quality to them. This is generally the go-to shape for organizing and structuring a design, especially when text is involved.
  • Triangles can be stable or unstable, depending on whether they’re sitting on their base or rotating in space, respectively. The former gives the impression of staying still and grounded, while the latter gives the impression of having been thrown or dropped. As such, they're the go-to shape for suggesting movement, action, progress, speed, or even aggression. Think of directional arrows, bows and arrows, or even the “play” button.
  • Circles and similar shapes are much more passive, fluid, graceful, and comforting than straight-edged shapes, and, depending on how you use them, can even give a sense of warmth or sometimes femininity. In graphic design, they are also the most versatile shape when it comes to representing both man-made and natural objects like wheels, glasses, the sun, eyeballs, flower pistils, and more.

3D Forms

Closed lines are simply not enough to create the illusion of a three-dimensional shape. To create one, you must incorporate the clever use of another element into the shape: either color or value.

Our three-dimensional world is not evenly lit, which results in shadows. These shadows allow us to perceive the object as three-dimensional. The simplest way to create these shadows on paper is to use different values to color an object: lighter values for the more illuminated parts of the object, and darker values for the shadows.

Another more interesting way to create this effect is not with different tints and shades of the same color, but to use different colors for lights and shadows. Generally, a warm color will appear brighter against a cool color of the same value, with yellow being the brightest.
