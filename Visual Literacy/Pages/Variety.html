Variety

Variety is the use of elements that contrast each other, or are different from each other. Variety is the opposite of unity, but not quite polar opposites. In fact, they rely on each other; variety makes a design interesting, while unity fits those various elements together into one cohesive work of art.

I whipped up this poster (Fig. 1) as an example of variety without unity (and also of terrible white space usage). A million different fonts, which come in several sizes and colors, litter the page in conjunction with a few haphazardly placed lines and shapes. Everything is juxtapositioned so close together (which is one way to achieve unity), and yet so disconnected from each other. Sure, it's interesting to look at, but it's very ugly. It also ascribes an air of incompetence and untrustworthiness to the creator of the poster (me) and the service I offer (a website). This is not only true for a poster about graphic design, however, but for a poster about anything. “The design of the poster is low in quality, so the good or service must also be low in quality,” the audience will assume, conscious or otherwise.

Here is a better version of the poster (Fig. 2). There is still variety in the colors, fonts and lines, and yet the elements have enough in common with each other to create one united poster that's nice to look at. Everything has soft edges: the text, the circles, the background, and the lines. “Visual Literacy” is the same size and font as “Quality Designs,” which creates a strong mental link between the two. The circles are juxtapositioned so close together that they overlap, further connecting the ideas in each circle.

The variety is created largely by the colors; everything is pink, blue or purple for the most part. Yet purple is an intermediate color between blue and red, or blue and pink, so the gradient of hues simultaneously creates variety and unity across the whole page. The curvy lines separating the web address from the rest of the content also adds variety, but the curviness still matches somewhat with the circles' curvatures.
