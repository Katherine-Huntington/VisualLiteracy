Lines

A line is a set of two or more points that are connected. A line can be straight or curved; thick or thin; finitely or infinitely long. Lines are arguably the most basic and important element of design, as they are virtually everywhere; they set the boundaries of shapes, divide lights and shadows, and constitute a variety of textures.

Use of Lines

Lines have various uses in graphic design, such as the following: organization, texturization, guiding the eye, providing movement, emphasizing, or conveying universal meanings.

  • Organization: Lines are often used to visually break down designs into smaller parts, usually to separate different types of content. For example, a line may separate a website's navigation bar from the rest of the website, or one block of text from another block.
  • Texturization: Different types of lines can create different types of textures, such as carpets, grass, hardwood floors, broken glass, etc.
  • Guiding the Eye: Lines, such as arrows, can point users to places where you want them to look.
  • Providing Movement: Lines can suggest movement or a lack thereof. Wavy lines suggest water or gracefulness, for example. Straight lines can suggest rigidity, stagnation, or steadfastness, while curved lines can make a design feel more dynamic or organic.
  • Emphasis: Using lines with thicknesses or curvatures that are different from surrounding lines can create different levels of emphasis in certain parts of your design. For example, bold, blocky letters can draw attention, while thin letters tend to go more under the radar.
  • Symbolism: Lines can sometimes be used in place of words to convey universal meanings. Wavy lines suggest water; dashed lines suggest travel or cutouts; scribbles suggest chaos or disorder. Arrows can tell you where to look or where to go.

Characteristics of Lines

  • Horizontal lines suggest motionlessness, rest, peace, stability, or even a horizon of some kind. They can also reinforce the viewer's urge to read a design from left to right, though turning the line into an arrow can easily influence a viewer to analyze from right to left.
  • Vertical lines are reaching and inspirational. They suggest tallness or grandeur, like that of a skyscraper or a church steeple. They can also introduce an upward movement to your design and are generally more energetic and dynamic than horizontal lines.
  • Diagonal and curvilinear lines are the most dynamic lines, tending to create more movement and tension. They suggest movement, fluidity, high energy, or inclines, and can move a viewer's eyes in a particular direction. Generally, the more curved or diagonal the line is, the more energetic it is.
  • Construction lines are measured, geometric, directional and angular, giving them an industrial or man-made quality.
  • Lines going in all sorts of directions suggest chaos and energy.
  • Thin lines generally feel more delicate, agile, or aerodynamic. They may also create a sense of refinement.
  • Thick lines are stronger, bolder, heavier; they're very good for emphasis.
  • Repeated lines can suggest cooperation or unity, whereas lines of different thicknesses, curvatures or directions aren't quite as united and yet can create a sense of rhythm.
