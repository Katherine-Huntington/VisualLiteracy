The Elements of Design

Line

A line is a set of two or more points that are connected. A line can be straight or curved; thick or thin; finitely or infinitely long. Lines can be used to guide the eye towards particular points on your design, and can also be used to create textures.

Color

Color can have many uses. They can create moods; convey lights, shadows, and depth; and emphasize particular focal points of a design. Designers often utilize color wheels and color schemes to create effective color combinations.

Value

This element goes hand in hand with color; value is the tint, shade or tone of a color. Values can be used to convey light, shadows, depth and mass.

Space

Space can be broken down into black (or positive) space and white (or negative) space. Black space refers to space taken up by objects, and white space refers to “unused” space. Overusing one or the other can make your design look crowded or empty, so it's important to have a nice balance of both.

Shape

A shape is essentially a line whose ends meet together. A form is basically a three-dimensional shape and the way it occupies a space. Form can be imitated on a flat surface by utilizing elements such as color, value, space, and even other surrounding objects.

Texture

Texture is how something feels, or how something looks like it would feel. Texture can be created by using different lines, values or shapes.

Time

A design can appear to be dynamic (that is, to move) or stagnant (to not move). A dynamic image will appear as though someone had pressed the pause button, whereas with a stagnant image, you wouldn't be able to tell if time was frozen or not.
