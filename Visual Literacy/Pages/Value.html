Value

Value refers to the shade or tint of a color, with white being the highest possible value and black being the lowest. Playing with values can create all sorts of effects involving contrast, depth, and even mood.

Using Value

Value is the perceived brightness of a hue, which is determined by how much black or white is in the hue. Adding black to a hue makes it a shade; white makes it a tint, and gray makes it a tone. For instance, maroon is a shade of red, and pink is a tint of red.

If you don't want to use different tints, shades, or tones of a color, but you still want to create the illusion of luminosity, you can achieve a similar effect by using different hues. Even if all of the colors you use in a design are the same value, some colors will naturally appear brighter than others. Yellow is the brightest-looking hue, and violet is the darkest-looking. Generally speaking, warm colors will appear brighter than cool colors. It's also important to note that the perceived value of a color depends on the colors that surround it; a color will appear more luminous when surrounded by darker colors and less luminous when surrounded by lighter colors.

Value Ranges

Generally, there are three major types of value ranges that can have different effects on your design and determine how illuminated your design looks. You can even use several range types in different parts of your design to add complexity.

Light values, also known as tints, tend to feel warmer, friendlier, softer, and even sweeter. Dark values, or shades, don't quite have the opposite effect of tints, but rather have a wide range of effects: they may make a design look more mellow, more mysterious, more sophisticated, more down-to-earth, etc. The effect of a tint or a shade depends on the color of that tint or shade.

A wide range that includes light values against significantly darker values, or vise versa, creates a lot of contrast and is great for bolder, more dynamic designs. However, it can make for pretty interesting stagnant designs, as well. If an object's value contrasts highly with the value or values of its surroundings, the object becomes a focal point. Stark value differences can also be used to balance your design, or even to unbalance it, depending on your intentions.

A limited range, on the other hand, creates very little contrast, but at the same time it adds a sense of subtlety to your design. A low key range (that is, a range consisting of dark values) tends to evoke feelings of mystery, drama, dread, or even physical sensations such as cold or sleepiness. On the other hand, a high key range (a range of light values) feels more lightweight, lighthearted, and warm, and tends to evoke positive emotions.
