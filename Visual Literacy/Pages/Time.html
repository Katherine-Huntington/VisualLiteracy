Time

The dynamics of a design refers to how much it appears to move. Stationary designs appear to hold still, while dynamic designs appear to be full of movement. Most of the time, a design may be somewhere on a spectrum between the two. Sometimes, different aspects of a single design can differ in dynamics. Neither type of design is inherently good or bad, nor are their effects; it all depends on the purpose of the design.

The dynamics of a design is determined by how stable the elements appear to be. In other words, it’s determined by the apparent kinetic energy an element has. A ball sitting in the middle of a table is pretty stationary; the ball is sitting still and therefore has no kinetic energy. A ball that’s tipping over the edge of a table is more dynamic; that ball appears to have more kinetic energy and is therefore less stable. A ball that is falling off the edge is even more dynamic, because the ball is moving even faster than it was when it was still on the table.

Stationary designs tend to evoke feelings such as:

  • - calmness, serenity, peacefulness
  • - quiet, silence
  • - order, stability, reliability, predictability
  • - tedium, stagnancy, being stuck
  • - low energy, lethargy
  • - lifelessness
  • - abandonment, loneliness
  • - non-imminence

Dynamic designs tend to evoke feelings such as:

  • - high energy, excitement
  • - loudness, noisiness
  • - disorder, instability, unpredictability
  • - agility, speed
  • - liveliness, bustling
  • - imminent danger

The following techniques can be used to create the illusion of motion. If you do not want a dynamic design, then avoid using these.

Multiplication of Images

Sometimes you might show the progression of an action or a series of different motions by using several images of the subject in different locations (where the subject is in its environment) and/or positions (the subject's pose, posture or configuration). Blurs are often used in conjunction with this technique, but not always.

  • Fig. 1: The Tiktok logo is the same image (with the same "position") in three slightly different locations. The overlap of the red and blue music notes creates the illusion of vibration. Some people may even “hear” the vibration, like the kind that would come from a loudspeaker.
  • Fig. 2: Multiple frames taken of the subject are placed on top of one another, allowing us to imagine the motion of the swing. The subject is in the same location but a different position.
  • Fig. 3: The same bird is shown in different stages of taking flight. Notice how each stage is in a different location on the screen, as well as a different position.

Implied Motion

“Implied Motion” is an image or artwork that suggests that motion is in the process of occurring, like someone pressed the pause button on an action-packed movie scene. The viewer might even anticipate what is “going to happen” in the artwork.

Other Ways to Make a Dynamic Design

  • Wavering thicknesses can make a line more dynamic than consistent thickness.
  • Different sizes make a set of shapes more dynamic than similar sizes.
  • "Disorderly" placement makes it appear as though an object or set of objects are moving, as opposed to orderly placement.
  • Vibrant colors seem more energetic than muted colors do
